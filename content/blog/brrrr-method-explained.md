The **BRRRR method** — Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat — is one of the most powerful strategies in real estate investing because it lets you recycle the same pool of capital across deal after deal. Instead of tying up cash in a single rental property forever, the BRRRR method is built to give most (sometimes all) of your money back after each project, so you can go buy the next one. Done right, it is how investors go from one rental to a five, ten, or twenty-property portfolio without needing a fresh chunk of savings for every purchase.

This guide breaks down each step, shows where a bridge or fix-and-flip-style loan fits the front end and a DSCR loan fits the refinance, and walks through a numeric example so you can see how the capital actually recycles.

## What the BRRRR Method Actually Means

At its core, the BRRRR method is a repeatable system for turning distressed properties into cash-flowing rentals while pulling your capital back out along the way. Each letter is a distinct phase:

- **Buy** a property below market value, usually something distressed or dated that most buyers overlook.
- **Rehab** it to force appreciation — raising the property's value through renovation rather than waiting on the market.
- **Rent** it to a qualified tenant to establish stable, verifiable income.
- **Refinance** into a long-term loan based on the new, higher value, pulling your original capital back out.
- **Repeat** the process with that recovered capital on your next deal.

The magic is in the gap between what you spend and what the stabilized property is worth. When your all-in cost (purchase + rehab + carrying costs) lands well below the after-repair value (ARV), the refinance can return most of your investment. That recovered cash becomes the down payment on the next property.

## Step 1: Buy Below Market

Every BRRRR deal is won or lost at purchase. Your goal is to acquire a property at a meaningful discount to what it will be worth once renovated. That usually means distressed listings, off-market deals, auctions, tired rentals, or inherited properties where the seller values speed and certainty over squeezing out the last dollar.

A common screen is a version of the 70% rule: keep your all-in basis at or below roughly 70–75% of ARV. If a property will appraise for $250,000 rehabbed, you want your purchase price plus rehab to stay near or under $175,000–$187,500. That built-in equity is what makes the refinance work later.

This is also where fast financing matters. Sellers of distressed property take the buyer who can close, not the one with the best-sounding offer. A [fix and flip](/loans) or [bridge loan](/loans/bridge) lets you compete like a cash buyer and close in as few as 5 days, because these are business-purpose loans underwritten on the deal and the property — not tax returns or W-2s.

## Step 2: Rehab to Force Value

The rehab phase is where you manufacture equity. Unlike a flip aimed at retail buyers, a BRRRR rehab is built for tenants and durability: think solid kitchens and baths, hard-wearing flooring, fresh paint, updated fixtures, and mechanicals that won't generate midnight maintenance calls. Over-improving for the neighborhood ties up cash you're trying to recover.

Build a detailed scope of work with your contractor before you close so you know the number and the timeline. On the funding side, this is a real advantage of the right lender: at The Capital Cove, the acquisition is funded up front and 100% of the rehab budget is reimbursed in draws as work is completed. You front a smaller slice, and the renovation capital is released against progress — keeping more of your cash available for the deal.

Because the loan is interest-only during the project, every month counts. The faster you finish and stabilize, the less you pay in carrying costs and the sooner you can refinance.

## Step 3: Rent to a Qualified Tenant

Before any lender will refinance a BRRRR property, it needs to be *stabilized* — occupied by a paying tenant at a market rent. This step does two things: it produces income, and it proves the property performs as a rental.

Price the rent to the market and screen tenants carefully. The number that matters most for your refinance is whether the rent comfortably covers the future loan payment plus taxes, insurance, and reserves. That relationship — income versus debt — is exactly what the next step is built on.

## Step 4: Refinance Into a DSCR Loan

Here is where the capital comes back. Once the property is rented and seasoned, you refinance out of the short-term acquisition loan into a long-term loan based on the new appraised value. Because your rehab forced the value up, the new loan can be sized to return most (sometimes all) of your original cash.

For investment property held in an entity, the tool of choice is a [rental / DSCR loan](/loans/rental). DSCR stands for Debt-Service Coverage Ratio, and it means the loan qualifies on the property's rental income relative to its payment — not your personal income. If the rent covers the payment (typically a DSCR of 1.0 or higher), the property can qualify on its own merits. No tax returns, no employment verification, no debt-to-income gymnastics.

A cash-out refinance at, say, 75% of the new value pays off your bridge loan and returns your down payment and rehab capital. What's left is a tenant-occupied rental producing monthly cash flow — financed long-term, with your original investment freed up.

## Step 5: Repeat

Take the capital the refinance handed back and roll it into the next acquisition. Each cycle adds a cash-flowing property to your portfolio while keeping your working capital intact. That is the entire point of the BRRRR method: your money does the work more than once.

## A Worked BRRRR Example

Numbers make it concrete. Here's a simplified but realistic single-family BRRRR deal.

The acquisition and rehab:

- Purchase price: **$150,000**
- Rehab budget: **$40,000**
- All-in project cost: **$190,000**
- After-repair value (ARV): **$260,000**

Using a bridge/fix-and-flip loan, suppose you put down 15% of the purchase ($22,500), the lender funds the rest of the acquisition, and the $40,000 rehab is reimbursed in draws. Add roughly $8,000 in interest, insurance, and closing and carrying costs over a 6-month project.

Your cash into the deal is about **$30,500** ($22,500 down plus about $8,000 carrying) — because the rehab draws are reimbursed, not paid out of pocket up front.

The refinance:

- New appraised value: **$260,000**
- Cash-out DSCR refinance at 75% of value: **$195,000** new loan
- That $195,000 pays off the short-term acquisition loan and returns your invested cash.

With the property appraising at $260,000 and a $195,000 refinance, you've recovered essentially all of your roughly $30,500 in cash — and you now own a rental worth $260,000 with a tenant in place. If the market rent is $2,100 per month and the new payment plus taxes and insurance runs around $1,750, you're cash-flowing while your capital rolls into deal number two.

## Where BRRRR Deals Go Wrong

The strategy is powerful, but it isn't automatic. The most common ways investors get stuck:

- **Overpaying at purchase.** Thin equity means the refinance leaves cash trapped in the deal.
- **Blowing the rehab budget.** Every dollar over budget is a dollar you may not recover.
- **A low appraisal.** If the property doesn't appraise at your projected ARV, the cash-out shrinks. Be conservative with your ARV comps.
- **Slow execution.** Carrying costs stack up every month you hold the short-term loan.
- **Weak rent.** DSCR refinances hinge on rent covering the payment, so buy in real rental markets.

Conservative underwriting on the front end — realistic ARV, honest rehab numbers, and a lender who moves fast — is what keeps a BRRRR on track.
