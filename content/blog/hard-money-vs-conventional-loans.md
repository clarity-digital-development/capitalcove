When you find a deal, the financing question is rarely "can I get a loan?" — it's "which loan fits this deal, and can it close before I lose the property?" The **hard money vs. conventional loans** decision comes down to a trade-off between speed and cost. Conventional financing is cheaper but slow and paperwork-heavy. Hard money is more expensive per month but fast, flexible, and built around the deal rather than your W-2. Pick the wrong one and you either overpay or miss the opportunity entirely.

This guide breaks down the head-to-head differences for **investment property** — non-owner-occupied, business-purpose deals held in an entity — so you can match the right tool to the right project. (Note: everything here is about investment loans, not consumer homeowner mortgages.)

## Hard money vs. conventional loans: the 30-second answer

Here's the short version before we go deep:

- **Choose hard money** when the deal is time-sensitive, the property needs work, you're funding a renovation, or you can't document income the way a bank wants. This is the tool for fix and flips, auctions, and bridge situations.
- **Choose a conventional loan** when the property is already in good condition, you plan to hold it for years, and you have the time (30–60+ days) and clean personal financials to satisfy an underwriter.

Most active flippers use hard money to *acquire and renovate*, then refinance into cheaper long-term debt — or sell — once the property is stabilized. The two aren't rivals so much as different stages of the same playbook.

## Speed: days vs. weeks

Speed is the single biggest reason investors reach for hard money.

A conventional mortgage runs through a full institutional underwriting pipeline: income verification, employment checks, debt-to-income calculations, appraisal, and often a loan committee. Even in a smooth scenario you're looking at **30 to 60 days** from application to funding — and any hiccup (a self-employed borrower, a property condition issue, a rate change) can push it further.

Hard money is built for the opposite priority. Because approval is based on the property and the plan rather than a deep dive into your personal life, the timeline collapses. At **The Capital Cove**, deals can close in **as few as 5 days**. We've taken a first-time investor from application to a funded deal in 14 days and gotten another borrower clear-to-close in 5.

That speed is not a luxury — it's leverage. Sellers of distressed and off-market property accept lower offers from buyers who can actually close fast. Auction properties often *require* proof of funds and a closing within days. If you're competing on a good flip, a slow lender is a dealbreaker, not an inconvenience.

## Qualification: the asset vs. your income and DTI

This is the fundamental philosophical split between the two.

**Conventional loans qualify the borrower.** Underwriters want to know your income, your two-year employment history, your debt-to-income ratio, and how many other financed properties you already carry (many programs cap you at four to ten). If you're self-employed, a full-time investor, or writing off income on your tax returns, this is exactly where deals die — the paperwork says you "don't make enough" even when your bank account says otherwise.

**Hard money qualifies the deal.** The core questions are: What is the property worth now? What will it be worth after repairs (the ARV)? Does the numbers add up? Your credit still matters for pricing and eligibility, but the property is the star of the show.

Practically, that means our fix and flip program has requirements that would surprise a bank borrower:

- **No experience required** — first-time investors are welcome.
- **Minimum credit score of 640** — not the 700+ many conventional investor programs want.
- **Bank-statement only** — no tax returns, no W-2s, no income verification.
- **Entity required** — you borrow through an LLC or other entity, because these are business-purpose loans.

## Documentation: a shoebox vs. a filing cabinet

The qualification difference shows up directly in how much paper you push.

A conventional investment-property loan typically wants tax returns (often two years), W-2s or 1099s, pay stubs, bank statements, a schedule of real estate owned, explanations for any credit inquiries, and more. Every document is a chance for the file to stall.

Hard money strips that down dramatically. For our fix and flip loans, we work from **bank statements** and the deal itself — property details, your scope of work and rehab budget, and the entity paperwork. No tax returns. No W-2s. No income verification. Less documentation is a big part of *why* the timeline is measured in days instead of months.

## Rates, points, and how the cost really compares

There's no way around it: hard money carries a higher interest rate. But comparing the headline rates alone is misleading, and it's where a lot of investors think about cost the wrong way.

Conventional investment-property mortgages price lower — think mid-single-digit territory for a well-qualified borrower — because they're long-term, fully amortizing loans on stabilized property. Hard money at **The Capital Cove** runs **9% to 11.5%, interest-only**, plus points (origination fees) typical of short-term bridge capital.

Here's the reframe: a flip loan is only outstanding for a few months, and it's *interest-only*, so you're not paying down principal on money you'll return quickly. A rate difference that looks enormous as an annual number is a small line item over a 5-month hold — and it's trivial compared to the profit you make by closing fast and getting the renovation funded. (You'll notice we never quote an "APR" on these; that's a consumer-mortgage concept, and these are business-purpose investment loans.)

The features that actually protect your returns are structural:

- **Up to 75% of ARV** — leverage against the finished value, not just the purchase price.
- **100% of the rehab budget funded in draws** — you're not floating the renovation out of pocket.
- **No prepayment penalty** — sell or refinance the day the project is done without a fee for winning early.
- **12–24 month terms** with a **$100k minimum loan** — sized for real projects.

## Term length: short-term tool vs. long-term hold

Conventional mortgages are marathon debt: 15- to 30-year terms, fully amortizing, designed for a property you intend to own for a long time. That's ideal for a stabilized rental.

Hard money is a **short-term tool** — 12 to 24 months, interest-only, meant to get in, add value, and get out. Using a 30-year mortgage to fund a 6-month renovation is the wrong shape of money (and most banks won't lend on a property that needs significant work anyway). Using hard money to hold a rental for a decade would be needlessly expensive. Matching the term to the strategy is half the battle.

## Best use-cases: matching the loan to the deal

To make it concrete, here's when each tool wins.

**Reach for hard money when:**

1. You're doing a **fix and flip** and need to buy and renovate quickly.
2. The property is **distressed** or needs work — conventional lenders shy away from condition issues.
3. You're buying at **auction** or off-market and must close in days.
4. You need a **[bridge loan](/loans/bridge)** to move on a new property before an existing one sells.
5. Your **income is hard to document** the conventional way (self-employed, full-time investor, heavy write-offs).

**Reach for a conventional loan when:**

1. The property is **already stabilized** and in good condition.
2. You're buying a **long-term rental** and want the lowest long-term rate — though note that a [DSCR rental loan](/loans/rental) often beats a conventional mortgage for investors, because it qualifies on the property's cash flow instead of your personal DTI.
3. You have **plenty of time** to close and **clean, well-documented personal financials**.

## The two work together

The savviest investors don't treat this as an either/or. The classic play: use hard money to acquire and renovate fast, then either sell the flip or refinance into long-term financing once the property is stabilized and appraising at the higher value. Hard money buys you speed and access; conventional (or DSCR) debt gives you cheap, patient capital to hold. Knowing which one to deploy at each stage is what separates investors who close deals from those who watch them go to someone faster.
