A **hard money loan** is a short-term, asset-based loan secured by real estate and funded by a private lender rather than a bank. Instead of underwriting your personal income the way a mortgage lender does, a hard money lender underwrites the *deal itself* — the property's current value, its after-repair value (ARV), and whether your investment plan makes sense. For real estate investors who need to move fast on distressed or undervalued property, a hard money loan is often the difference between winning a deal and watching someone else close it.

At The Capital Cove — a private lender founded by an active investor, not a bank committee — we write these loans every week for fix and flip projects across 47 states. This guide breaks down exactly what a hard money loan is, how it differs from a bank loan, what the terms and pricing look like, the real pros and cons, and what lenders actually look for before they fund.

## What Is a Hard Money Loan, Really?

The name sounds intimidating, but the concept is simple. "Hard money" refers to money lent against a *hard asset* — in this case, real estate. The property is the collateral, and the property is the primary basis for approval.

These are **business-purpose loans**. They are made to an entity (an LLC), for non-owner-occupied investment property that you intend to renovate, rent, or resell. They are not consumer mortgages, and you can't use one to buy a home you plan to live in. That distinction matters because it's what lets a hard money loan skip most of the bureaucracy attached to traditional home financing.

Because the loan is tied to the asset and the exit strategy — flip it, refinance it, or sell it — the lender cares far more about the numbers of the deal than about your W-2 or your debt-to-income ratio. That's the entire reason investors use them.

## Hard Money vs. Bank Loans: Asset-Based vs. Income-Based

The clearest way to understand a hard money loan is to contrast it with a conventional bank loan.

- **What gets underwritten.** A bank underwrites *you* — your income, tax returns, employment history, and credit depth. A hard money lender underwrites the *property* — purchase price, rehab budget, ARV, and the strength of your plan.
- **Documentation.** Banks want two years of tax returns, W-2s, pay stubs, and endless paperwork. At The Capital Cove, fix and flip loans are bank-statement only — no tax returns, no W-2s, no income verification.
- **Speed.** A bank purchase can take 30 to 60 days to close. Hard money can close in as few as 5 days, which is why investors use it to win competitive and auction deals.
- **Property condition.** Banks typically won't lend on a property that needs significant work — a leaking roof or a gutted kitchen fails their appraisal. Hard money lenders *expect* the property to need work; the rehab is the whole point.
- **Term length.** Bank mortgages run 15 to 30 years. Hard money loans are short — typically 12 to 24 months — because they're bridge financing for a project, not permanent debt.

In short, a bank loan is income-based and slow but cheap. A hard money loan is asset-based, fast, and flexible, and you pay a premium for that speed and flexibility. For a flip that you'll own for six months, that trade is usually well worth it.

## Typical Hard Money Terms, Rates, and Points

Pricing on a hard money loan reflects the short term and the higher-touch nature of the lending. Here's what current terms look like on our [fix and flip program](/loans):

- **Interest rates:** 9% to 11.5%, interest-only. You pay interest on the money you've drawn, and there's no big amortized principal payment eating your monthly cash flow.
- **Leverage:** Up to 75% of ARV, with 100% of the rehab budget funded in draws as you complete the work.
- **Minimum loan:** $100,000.
- **Term:** 12 to 24 months — enough runway to buy, renovate, and sell without pressure.
- **Points / origination:** Most hard money loans carry an origination fee (commonly called "points") charged at closing. One point equals 1% of the loan amount.
- **Minimum credit:** 640, and no prior experience is required — first-time investors are welcome.
- **No prepayment penalty:** Sell or refinance early and you stop paying interest. You're never penalized for a fast, profitable flip.

A note on language: because these are interest-only, business-purpose loans and not consumer mortgages, you won't see an "APR" quoted the way you would on a home loan. You'll evaluate the deal on the rate, the points, and the total carrying cost over your projected hold. ## How Rehab Draws Work

One feature that confuses first-timers is the **draw structure**. When we fund 100% of your rehab budget, that money isn't handed over on day one. It's held in reserve and released in stages — "draws" — as you complete portions of the work.

You finish demo and rough-in, request a draw, an inspection confirms the work, and the funds are released to reimburse you. This protects both sides: the lender knows the money is going into the property, and you only pay interest on rehab funds once they're actually drawn. It also keeps your project on a disciplined timeline.

## The Pros and Cons of Hard Money Loans

No financing tool is right for every situation. Here's an honest look.

**Pros:**

1. **Speed.** Closings in days, not months — essential for auctions, wholesale deals, and any seller who wants certainty.
2. **Deal-based approval.** Approval hinges on the property and plan, not your day job. First-time investors and self-employed borrowers who can't easily document income can still qualify.
3. **Rehab funding.** Getting 100% of the renovation covered in draws keeps enormous amounts of your own cash free.
4. **Flexibility.** Private lenders can structure around unusual situations that a bank's checklist would reject outright.
5. **Leverage.** Financing up to 75% of ARV lets you control more property with less capital in the deal.

**Cons:**

1. **Higher rates.** You'll pay more than a 30-year mortgage rate — that's the cost of speed and flexibility.
2. **Short terms.** You need a clear, realistic exit within 12 to 24 months.
3. **Points and fees.** Upfront origination costs should be built into your deal math.
4. **Discipline required.** Because you're paying interest while you hold, delays are expensive. Time is money on a flip.

## When Should You Use a Hard Money Loan?

A hard money loan is the right tool when speed, property condition, or documentation make a bank loan impractical. The most common scenarios:

- **Fix and flip projects** where you buy distressed, renovate, and resell within a year.
- **Bridge financing** when you need to close on a new property before an existing one sells. (See our [bridge loan program](/loans/bridge).)
- **Auction and wholesale deals** with hard closing deadlines.
- **Properties that won't pass a bank appraisal** because they need too much work.
- **BRRRR strategies**, where you use short-term hard money to buy and rehab, then refinance into a longer-term [rental / DSCR loan](/loans/rental) once the property is stabilized.

If your plan is to hold a turnkey rental for years, a DSCR loan is usually the better long-term fit. Hard money shines in the *acquisition and renovation* phase.

## What Hard Money Lenders Look For

When you submit a deal, an experienced lender is really evaluating four things:

1. **The purchase price relative to ARV.** Many investors follow the 70% rule — pay no more than 70% of ARV minus rehab costs — to build in margin.
2. **A realistic rehab budget and scope of work.** Vague numbers signal risk. A detailed, contractor-backed scope signals a prepared borrower.
3. **A clear exit strategy.** Selling? Refinancing? The lender wants to see how the loan gets repaid.
4. **Basic borrower fundamentals.** A 640-plus credit score, an LLC or entity to take title, and enough reserves to carry the project. Experience helps but isn't required — we've funded first-timers to a clear-to-close in as little as 5 days, and closed a first-time investor's deal in 14 days.
