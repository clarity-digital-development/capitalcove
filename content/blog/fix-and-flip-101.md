If you have found a run-down house, run the numbers, and realized you do not have enough cash to buy it and renovate it, you have hit the same wall almost every new investor hits. The answer most experienced flippers reach for is not a bank mortgage. It is a **fix and flip loan** — short-term, business-purpose financing built specifically for buying, renovating, and reselling an investment property held by an entity.

This guide walks you through how fix and flip loans actually work, what you need to bring to the table, and the specific mistakes that trip up first-timers. No jargon, no fluff — just the mechanics you need to fund your first deal with confidence.

One thing to get out of the way early: you do not need prior flips on your résumé to get funded. The first two deals The Capital Cove ever closed were both for first-time investors. What matters is whether the deal makes sense.

## What a Fix and Flip Loan Actually Is

A fix and flip loan is a short-term loan — usually 12 to 24 months — secured by the property you are renovating. It is designed for **non-owner-occupied investment property** that you buy through an LLC, fix up, and sell. This is a business-purpose loan, not a homeowner mortgage, which is exactly why it can move so much faster than a conventional bank.

The core difference from a traditional mortgage is what the lender underwrites. A bank scrutinizes *you* — your W-2s, your tax returns, your debt-to-income ratio. A fix and flip lender underwrites *the deal* — what the property is worth today, what it will be worth after the renovation, and whether your plan is realistic.

Because the loan is asset-based, a few things follow naturally:

- **Speed.** With less personal paperwork to verify, closings happen in days, not months. Our fastest fix and flip loans have closed in as few as 5 days.
- **Interest-only payments.** You pay interest only during the project, which keeps your monthly carrying cost low while you renovate.
- **Documentation is light.** A bank-statement review, not a stack of tax returns. No W-2s, no income verification.
- **Rates reflect the speed and risk.** Fix and flip loans run higher than a 30-year mortgage — ours range from 9% to 11.5% — because they are fast, short-term, and flexible. On a deal you hold for six months, the rate matters far less than the profit spread.

## The 70% Rule: How Much to Pay for the House

Before you talk to any lender, you need to know whether the deal is worth doing. The single most useful tool for that is the **70% rule**.

The rule is a quick guardrail: your all-in purchase price should be no more than 70% of the property's after-repair value (ARV), minus your estimated renovation costs.

The formula looks like this:

**Maximum offer = (ARV × 0.70) − rehab costs**

Here is a concrete example. Say comparable renovated homes in the neighborhood are selling for $300,000 — that is your ARV. You estimate the rehab at $50,000.

1. Multiply the ARV by 70%: $300,000 × 0.70 = $210,000
2. Subtract your rehab budget: $210,000 − $50,000 = $160,000

So your maximum purchase price is about **$160,000**. That roughly $90,000 gap between your all-in cost and the sale price is what has to cover your financing, holding costs, closing costs on both ends, agent commissions, and — what is left — your profit.

The 70% rule is a starting filter, not gospel. In a hot market with thin inventory, disciplined flippers sometimes stretch to 75%. On a first deal, treat 70% as a hard ceiling. It builds in the margin that protects you when the rehab runs long or the market softens. If you want to pressure-test a specific deal, run it through our free [Flip Profit Calculator](/resources/calculator) before you make an offer.

> The profit on a flip is made at the purchase, not the sale. Overpay for the house and no amount of good renovation work will save the deal.

## Down Payment and How the Money Is Structured

A common misconception is that a fix and flip loan covers everything. It does not — and understanding the structure is key to knowing how much cash you actually need.

Here is how a typical deal is funded:

- **Acquisition.** The lender finances most of the purchase price, and you bring a down payment. Plan on roughly **10–15% of the purchase price** out of pocket. On the $160,000 example above, that is around $16,000–$24,000.
- **Rehab.** This is the part that surprises first-timers in a good way. At The Capital Cove, **100% of the renovation budget is financed** and released to you in draws as the work gets done. You are not fronting $50,000 of rehab cash on top of the down payment.
- **Reserves.** Lenders want to see that you can carry the project. That means having enough in the bank to cover a few months of interest payments, insurance, and unexpected overruns. This is verified with a bank statement — no tax returns required.

Loans are capped two ways at once: a percentage of the after-repair value and the total project cost. Our fix and flip loans go **up to 75% of ARV**, with a **minimum loan amount of $100,000**. Knowing both caps up front tells you exactly how much cash you need to close.

## How Rehab Draws Work

The rehab draw is the mechanic new investors understand least, so it is worth slowing down on.

You do not receive your entire $50,000 rehab budget at closing. Instead, the renovation money sits in a holdback and is released in stages — called draws — as you complete the work. Here is the cycle:

1. You complete a phase of work (say, demo and framing).
2. You request a draw for that phase.
3. The lender sends an inspector to verify the work is actually done.
4. Once it passes, funds are released — typically within a couple of business days — to reimburse you and fund the next phase.

This is why reserves matter. You often pay your contractor for a phase and then get reimbursed on the draw, so you need working capital to keep the project moving between disbursements. Structure your contractor payments around the draw schedule and everyone stays happy.

The upside of the draw system is that you only pay interest on money that has actually been disbursed. During the early phases, your loan balance — and your interest cost — is lower.

## A Realistic Timeline for Your First Flip

Every deal is different, but a first flip usually breaks down something like this:

- **Days 1–5: Financing.** Submit the deal, get terms, upload documents, and close. A clean deal with title cooperating can [close in as few as 5 days](/how-it-works); the more common range is 10–14 days.
- **Weeks 1–2: Mobilize.** Contractor starts, permits pulled, materials ordered. Do not lose momentum here — holding costs are running whether work is happening or not.
- **Months 1–4: Renovate.** The actual rehab. Your timeline depends on scope, but most cosmetic-to-moderate flips run two to four months.
- **Months 4–6: List and sell.** A well-renovated home in a healthy market typically goes under contract within 30–60 days.

Because there is **no prepayment penalty** on our fix and flip loans, the day you sell, you pay off the loan and keep the spread — there is no penalty for finishing early. That alignment matters: the lender makes money when you move fast, and so do you.

## Common First-Timer Mistakes to Avoid

The deals that go sideways almost always trace back to a handful of avoidable errors:

- **Underestimating the rehab.** New flippers budget for what they can see and get blindsided by what they cannot — old wiring, a bad roof, foundation issues. Pad your rehab estimate by 10–15% for contingencies.
- **Over-improving for the neighborhood.** Installing $80,000 finishes in a $300,000 neighborhood does not raise your ARV — it just burns your profit. Renovate to the comps, not to your personal taste.
- **Ignoring holding costs.** Every month you own the property, interest, insurance, utilities, and taxes are eating into your margin. A rehab that drags an extra 90 days can quietly erase a chunk of profit.
- **A vague scope of work.** "Fix up the kitchen" is not a plan. Lenders — and contractors — need a line-item scope with costs. It is also what protects you from a contractor's mid-project "surprises."
- **Waiting for the perfect track record before starting.** You learn to flip by flipping. A solid deal with a realistic plan beats a perfect résumé.
