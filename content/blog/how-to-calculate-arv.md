If you learn one skill before you buy your first flip, make it this one: **how to calculate ARV**. After Repair Value (ARV) is your best estimate of what a property will sell for *after* you finish the renovation. Nearly every decision downstream flows from that number: your maximum purchase price, your rehab budget, your profit, and even how much a lender like The Capital Cove will fund. Get the ARV right and the rest of the deal tends to work. Get it wrong, and no amount of hustle on the rehab will save you.

This guide walks through exactly how to calculate ARV using comparable sales, why it drives loan sizing (we fund up to 75% of ARV), how the classic 70% rule uses ARV to keep you disciplined, a full worked example, and the mistakes that trip up first-time and experienced investors alike.

## What ARV Actually Means

ARV is not what the house is worth today, and it is not what you hope it will sell for. It is the realistic market value of the property once your planned renovations are complete and it is in the same condition as recently sold, comparable homes in the neighborhood.

Think of it as answering a single question: *"If I finished this rehab today and listed it, what would a buyer actually pay?"* The word "actually" is doing a lot of work there. ARV is grounded in closed sales, not list prices, not Zillow estimates, and not your optimism.

Because ARV is forward-looking, it always pairs with a scope of work. A property's ARV assumes a specific level of finish. If your comps are updated kitchens with quartz counters and refinished floors, your rehab has to deliver that level of finish to earn that value.

## Why ARV Drives Everything (Including Your Loan)

ARV is the anchor for the whole deal, and it directly determines how much financing you can get.

At The Capital Cove, our [fix & flip loans](/loans) are sized against ARV: we lend up to **75% of the After Repair Value**, and we fund **100% of the rehab budget** in draws as the work gets done. So if a property has a defensible $400,000 ARV, the loan can be sized up to roughly $300,000 against that value. That is why an accurate ARV is not just an analysis exercise, it is what unlocks (or limits) your capital.

ARV also feeds three other critical numbers:

- **Maximum purchase price** — the most you can pay and still hit your profit target
- **Total project budget** — purchase plus rehab plus holding and closing costs
- **Projected profit** — ARV minus every dollar you put in

An inflated ARV quietly inflates all three. You "afford" a higher purchase price, you justify a bigger rehab, and you pencil in a fat profit, all built on a number that will not hold up at resale. This is the most common way flips go from projected gain to real loss.

## How to Calculate ARV Using Comps

The reliable way to calculate ARV is the comparable sales method, the same approach appraisers use. Here is the step-by-step process.

1. **Pull recent sold comps.** Find 3 to 6 properties that have *sold and closed* (not pending, not listed) in the last 3 to 6 months. Recent closings reflect current market conditions. Older sales can mislead you in a moving market.

2. **Stay close geographically.** Ideally within a half-mile, and inside the same neighborhood, school zone, and subdivision. In dense urban areas, tighten that to a few blocks. Never cross a major boundary (a highway, a school district line, a rougher part of town) to grab a better number.

3. **Match the property type and size.** Compare single-family to single-family, and keep bedroom/bathroom count and square footage close, within about 20% of your subject's finished size. A 1,200 sq ft house is not comparable to a 2,400 sq ft house, even next door.

4. **Match condition and finish level.** This is the step that makes it "After Repair" Value. Your comps should be renovated homes in the condition you plan to deliver. Comparing your finished flip to tired, un-updated houses will understate your ARV, comparing it to luxury new builds will overstate it.

5. **Calculate price per square foot, then adjust.** Divide each comp's sold price by its finished square footage to get a price per square foot. Look at the range, throw out obvious outliers, and settle on a defensible figure. Then multiply by your subject's finished square footage. Adjust up or down for meaningful differences: an extra bathroom, a garage, a larger lot, a pool, a superior view.

For example, if three strong renovated comps sold at $195, $205, and $200 per square foot, you might reasonably use $200. On a 2,000 sq ft subject, that points to an ARV around $400,000, before fine-tuning for features your comps have and yours does not (or vice versa).

The goal is a number you could defend to an appraiser, a partner, or a lender with the actual sold comps in hand. If you cannot back it up with closed sales, it is a wish, not an ARV.

## The 70% Rule: Turning ARV Into a Max Offer

Once you have a solid ARV, the **70% rule** turns it into a disciplined maximum purchase price. The formula:

> Maximum Allowable Offer = (ARV × 0.70) − Estimated Rehab Costs

The 70% is not magic. It is a buffer, roughly 30% of ARV, that has to cover your holding costs, closing costs on both ends, financing costs, agent commissions, and your profit margin. Subtract your rehab estimate and you are left with the most you can pay for the property.

Let's run the numbers on our $400,000 ARV example with a $60,000 rehab:

- ARV: $400,000
- ARV × 0.70 = $280,000
- Minus rehab of $60,000
- **Maximum offer: $220,000**

If you can get the property at or below $220,000, the deal has room to absorb surprises and still turn a profit. If the seller wants $260,000, the 70% rule is telling you to walk, or to find a way to raise the ARV (a better scope) or cut the rehab. The rule protects you from talking yourself into a thin deal.

The 70% rule is a starting screen, not gospel. In hot, low-inventory markets, experienced investors sometimes stretch to 75%. On heavier, riskier rehabs or slower markets, tightening to 65% builds in more cushion. Adjust the percentage to the risk, but always anchor it to a real ARV. ## Worked Example: A Full ARV Analysis

Let's put it all together on a realistic deal.

You find a dated 3-bed, 2-bath, 1,800 sq ft house. You pull four renovated comps in the same subdivision, all sold in the last four months:

- Comp A: 1,850 sq ft, sold $370,000 → $200/sq ft
- Comp B: 1,750 sq ft, sold $355,000 → $203/sq ft
- Comp C: 1,900 sq ft, sold $380,000 → $200/sq ft
- Comp D: 1,800 sq ft, sold $410,000 → $228/sq ft (has a pool your subject lacks)

Comp D is an outlier driven by the pool, so you set it aside for your base figure and use roughly **$200/sq ft**. On 1,800 sq ft, that gives an **ARV of $360,000**.

Now apply the 70% rule with a $55,000 rehab:

- $360,000 × 0.70 = $252,000
- Minus $55,000 rehab = **$197,000 maximum offer**

Say you get it under contract at $190,000. Here is how The Capital Cove could structure it: with a $360,000 ARV, a loan up to 75% of ARV supports up to about $270,000 in total financing, and we fund 100% of that $55,000 rehab in draws as the work passes inspection. That means far less of your own cash tied up in the project, and the ARV you defended with real comps is exactly what made that loan amount possible.

## Common ARV Mistakes That Sink Deals

Even a good process breaks down if you fall into these traps:

- **Using list prices or estimates instead of sold comps.** Asking prices and automated valuations reflect hope and algorithms, not what buyers paid. Only closed sales count.
- **Reaching for comps that are too far away or too different.** Crossing a neighborhood boundary or comparing different property types inflates the number. Tight and similar beats far and pretty.
- **Ignoring finish level.** If your comps have quartz, luxury vinyl plank, and updated baths, your rehab has to match. Underbuilding the scope means you will not hit the ARV your comps imply.
- **Using stale comps.** In a shifting market, sales from eight or nine months ago can be off by tens of thousands. Stay inside 3 to 6 months.
- **Cherry-picking the highest comp.** One aggressive sale is not the market. Use the cluster, discard true outliers, and be honest.
- **Forgetting to adjust for features.** A comp with a garage, extra bath, or bigger lot is not apples-to-apples. Adjust before you rely on it.
- **Confusing "as-is" value with ARV.** ARV assumes the work is done. The current, un-renovated value is a separate number, do not mix them up.

The single best habit is conservatism. When in doubt, use the lower defensible ARV. A deal that works at a conservative ARV is a deal that works in the real world. A deal that only works at your most optimistic number is a deal that only works on a spreadsheet.
