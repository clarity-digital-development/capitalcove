If you're about to fund your first (or fifth) flip, the smartest thing you can do before you make an offer is understand the **fix and flip loan requirements** you'll actually be judged on. The good news: business-purpose flip lending is far more flexible than a traditional mortgage. There are no tax returns, no W-2s, and no income verification — and at The Capital Cove, no prior experience is required. What matters is your credit, your entity, the money you bring to the table, and whether the deal itself makes sense.

Below is a clear, honest walkthrough of every fix and flip loan requirement, why each one exists, and what "good enough" looks like in practice. Knowing this ahead of time is the difference between a smooth 5-day close and a stalled deal.

## The 6 core fix and flip loan requirements

Most private lenders — us included — underwrite a flip loan against six things. Get these lined up and you're in strong shape:

1. **Credit** — a middle score of 640 or higher.
2. **A business entity** — an LLC or corporation to hold the loan.
3. **Skin in the game** — a down payment on the purchase, typically 10–15%.
4. **The deal** — a realistic scope of work and a defensible after-repair value (ARV).
5. **Reserves** — cash left over after closing to carry the project.
6. **Documentation** — bank statements, the purchase contract, and entity paperwork.

Let's take each one in turn.

## 1. Credit: 640 minimum, no perfection required

Credit is the first fix and flip loan requirement most borrowers worry about, and it's usually less of a hurdle than they expect. At The Capital Cove, the minimum credit score is **640** (we use the middle of your three bureau scores). We're not looking for an 800 — we're checking that you handle obligations responsibly and don't have active, unresolved problems like a recent bankruptcy or open collections tied to lending.

Your score influences where you land inside our rate range of **9%–11.5% interest-only**, but a 640 does not disqualify you. If you're sitting just under the line, a few weeks of paying down revolving balances and disputing errors can often push you over. Because these are business-purpose loans on investment property, your credit is one data point — not the whole decision.

## 2. A business entity (LLC or corporation)

This one is non-negotiable, and it surprises first-timers: **the loan must be made to an entity, not to you personally.** Fix and flip loans are business-purpose loans for non-owner-occupied investment property, so the borrower on the note is your LLC or corporation, not your name alone.

Why it's required:

- It keeps the loan clearly on the business-purpose side of the line (these are not consumer mortgages).
- It creates a layer of liability protection between the project and your personal assets.
- It's how serious investors operate — lenders, title companies, and insurers all expect it.

You don't need a decade-old company with employees. A single-member LLC formed specifically to hold the deal is perfectly fine. Setting one up in most states takes a day or two and a modest filing fee. You'll typically provide the Articles of Organization, your Operating Agreement, and an EIN. If you don't have an entity yet, form it early — it's the one requirement that can add days to your timeline if you leave it to the last minute. ## 3. Down payment and "skin in the game"

No reputable lender funds 100% of a purchase price, and you should be cautious of anyone who claims to. The point of a down payment is alignment: when you have your own capital in the deal, your interests and the lender's point the same direction.

Here's how the math actually works on a Capital Cove fix and flip loan:

- **Up to 75% of ARV** is the ceiling on total loan exposure (purchase + rehab combined).
- **100% of the rehab budget** is funded — but in draws, released as work is completed and inspected, not handed over at closing.
- **10–15% of the purchase price** is the down payment most borrowers bring.
- **Minimum loan amount is $100,000.**

An example. Say you're buying a property for $200,000 with a $60,000 rehab and an ARV of $360,000. We can lend up to 75% of that $360,000 ARV — about $270,000 — which comfortably covers the roughly $170,000 balance on the purchase (after your ~15% down) plus the full $60,000 rehab in draws. Your out-of-pocket down payment is around $30,000, and the rehab money comes back to you as you hit milestones. Run your own numbers on our free [Flip Profit Calculator](/resources/calculator) before you make an offer.

Because rehab is funded in draws, plan to **front the first phase of construction** and get reimbursed after inspection. That's normal, and it's why reserves (next) matter.

## 4. The deal itself: scope of work + ARV

You can have flawless credit and a clean LLC, but if the deal doesn't pencil out, it won't get funded — and that's a favor to you, not an obstacle. The property is the collateral, so the numbers on the project carry real weight in underwriting.

Two documents do most of the talking:

- **Scope of work (SOW):** an itemized rehab budget — what you're doing, line by line, and what it costs. A contractor's bid is ideal. Vague ("full reno, ~$50k") is a red flag; specific ("kitchen $18k, two baths $14k, roof $9k, paint/flooring $11k…") signals a borrower who has done the homework.
- **After-repair value (ARV):** the realistic sale price once the work is done, supported by recent comparable sales of similar renovated homes nearby — not aspirational Zillow guesses.

A quick sanity check underwriters and smart investors both use is the **70% rule**: your all-in cost (purchase + rehab) should stay at or below roughly 70% of ARV, leaving room for holding costs, selling costs, and profit. ## 5. Reserves: the requirement people forget

Reserves are the quiet fix and flip loan requirement that trips up under-capitalized borrowers. Beyond your down payment and closing costs, you need cash on hand after closing to actually carry the project.

Your loan is **interest-only**, so you'll make monthly interest payments during the 12–24 month term while the property produces no income. You'll also cover insurance, property taxes, utilities, and — critically — the **first draw of construction** before you're reimbursed. Overruns and surprise repairs happen on nearly every flip.

A practical target is enough liquidity to cover several months of carrying costs plus your initial rehab outlay. You don't have to document a huge bank balance, but showing reasonable reserves both strengthens your file and, more importantly, keeps your project from stalling halfway through. The investors who get burned are almost always the ones who budgeted for the purchase and forgot about the months in between.

## 6. Documentation: refreshingly light

Here's where business-purpose lending shines compared to a conventional loan. The documentation list is short, and there are **no tax returns, no W-2s, and no income verification** at The Capital Cove:

- **Bank statements** (typically 2–3 months) to confirm your down payment and reserves — bank-statement only, not income underwriting.
- **The signed purchase contract** for the property.
- **Entity documents** — Articles of Organization, Operating Agreement, and EIN.
- **Scope of work / rehab budget**, ideally a contractor bid.
- **Property details** and your ARV support (comps or an appraisal we order).
- **A government ID** and authorization to pull credit.

That's the whole picture. No stacks of pay stubs, no explaining a self-employed Schedule C, no waiting on a tax transcript. It's a big part of why we can move from application to clear-to-close in as few as 5 days — we've done it, and we've closed a first-time investor's very first deal in 14 days.

## What is *not* required

Just as important as the requirements are the things you might assume you need but don't:

- **Prior flipping experience.** First-timers are welcome. Both of the first deals we ever funded were for first-time investors, and we judge the deal on its merits.
- **Tax returns or proof of income.** These are asset- and deal-based loans, not income-based.
- **A perfect credit score.** 640 is the floor, not a target.
- **A prepayment penalty to worry about.** There isn't one — sell or refinance whenever the project is done. If you plan to keep the property as a rental, our [Rental / DSCR loans](/loans/rental) or a [bridge loan](/loans/bridge) can take you from flip to long-term hold.
